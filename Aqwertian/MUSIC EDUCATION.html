Music at Your Fingertips!  

in  Music Education


Aqwertyan Music can be a big aid in teaching the fundamentals of music! The basic concepts listed below are often taught successfully independent of learning to ‘finger’ a particular instrument.  In fact many schools in the United States, tight on budget, have had to forego their bands and orchestras in favor of computing devices.   However, now a qwerty keyboard, with the addition of velocity sensitivity offered by the  iPad,  has become a very worthy musical instrument in its own right.  


TEMPO

Tempo is the speed of music.  It’s a steady constant pulse,  like a clock ticking.  Tempo can be slow or fast or in-between, and it can change during a song.  Tempo influences how music sounds and feels.  The same piece of music will sound different if you play it slower or faster. 


RHYTHM       

If you listen for it, you  can find rhythm just about anywhere: In a basketball being dribbled, raindrops falling, or hands clapping.  Rhythm is what makes music move and flow. 


Rhythm is made up of sounds and silences.  These sounds and silences are put together to form patterns of sound, which are repeated to create rhythm.  A rhythm has a steady beat, but it may also have many different kinds of beats.   Some beats maybe stronger or longer or shorter or softer than others.  In a single piece of  music, composers often use many different rhythms. 


PITCH  

Music is made up of many sounds:  high sounds,  like birds chirping, low sounds, like thunder rolling over a valley, and medium sounds.


HARMONY 

To understand Harmony, you first need to  understand melody.  Melody is a group of 

notes played one after the other.  Melody is the tune.  It’s often the easiest part of music to remember.  It is  the part that you hum.


Harmony is also a group of notes, except these notes are played behind the melody.  Harmony supports the melody and gives the music texture or mood.  A piece of music can be made to sound totally different  just by changing the harmony.  The same melody will also sound very different if when played  with one harmony versus another. 

 

INSTRUMENTATION

In an orchestra, there are many instruments that make all kinds of different sounds.  Some, like violins, make higher sounds.  Others, like tubas, make lower sounds.


When playing or writing music, composers, conductors, and musicians have to decide what instruments will play what parts.   They also have to decide what instruments will play in combination with other instruments. That is what instrumentation is; deciding who plays what, with whom and when.
